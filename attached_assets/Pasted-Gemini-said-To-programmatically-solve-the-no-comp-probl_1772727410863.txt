1. The "Appraisal Context" Prompt Strategy
When your system detects a 1/1 (or any low-pop card with no recent sales), don't send a generic query. Use a structured prompt that forces the AI to use specific hobby logic (multipliers and proxies).

Your API prompt should look like this:

"I have a [Year] [Brand] [Player] [Parallel] (1/1). There are no direct sales. I am providing you with three datasets:

Vertical Comps: Recent sales of this player's /[5], /[10], and /[25] parallels from the same set.

Horizontal Comps: Recent sales of other 1/1s for players in the same 'tier' (e.g., if he's a top-10 rookie WR, give me other top-10 rookie WR 1/1s).

Brand Delta: The average price difference between this brand (e.g., Pro Set) and the premium brand (e.g., Prizm) for this player's common autos.

Task: Use a 2.5x–3.5x multiplier on the /[5] price and adjust based on the 'Brand Delta' to provide a defensible valuation."

2. The Data Pipeline (The "Backend" Logic)
To make this work, your app needs to pull more than just the scanned card's data. You should build a function that fetches "Tiered Comps":

Step A (Identify): Use Gemini's vision capability to confirm the card is a 1/1.

Step B (Fetch Surrounding Data): Hit your data provider (Card Ladder, eBay API, or 130Point) for the player's higher-numbered parallels (the /5s and /10s).

Step C (The Logic Layer): Pass that array of "surrounding data" into Gemini.

3. Implementing a "Defensibility" Score
To give your users confidence, have Gemini output its reasoning in a structured format that you can display in your UI.

Example UI Output:

In your system prompt for the app, include 3–5 examples of "Perfect Appraisals" where a 1/1 was priced correctly based on its /5 counterpart. This is called Few-Shot Prompting. It trains Gemini to recognize that a 1/1 isn't "priceless," but rather a mathematical extension of the /5 and /10 market.
