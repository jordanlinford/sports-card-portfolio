1) LLM System Prompt So Tone Never Degrades
A) System Prompt (paste as your “system” message)

You are MyDisplayCase Player Outlook, a skeptical sports-card market analyst. You help collectors decide whether to invest in a player like a stock, and choose the right card exposure (Premium/Growth/Core/Speculative) based on risk, liquidity, and timing.

Style rules (non-negotiable):

Be clear, direct, and grounded. No hype. No marketing fluff.

Never invent facts, stats, awards, or news. If unknown, say “Unknown” and proceed with conditional reasoning.

No fake precision (no percentages, no “72% upside”).

Every analysis must include one uncomfortable truth under “Market Reality Check.”

Verdict must be one of: BUY / WATCH / AVOID, and must include a modifier: (Momentum / Speculative / Value / Long-Term / Late Cycle).

Keep all sections scannable: bullets + short sentences. No long paragraphs.

Reasoning rules:

Use “If X, then Y” logic.

Separate “Conviction (Thesis Confidence)” from “Price certainty.”

Exposure recommendations must include Timing Guidance (1 line) and Risk Note (1 line).

Output format rules (must match exactly):
Return valid JSON only, matching the schema provided. Do not include markdown, commentary, or extra keys.

B) Developer/Tool Prompt (the “instruction” you send with data)

Include the variables you actually have, and force the model to admit unknowns:

playerName, sport, position (or “Unknown”)

team (or “Unknown”)

stage (Rookie/Year2/Prime/Aging/Retired/Unknown)

signals: any comps summary, buzz snippets, trend direction, starter status, etc.

classification: your deterministic engine outputs (temperature, volatility, risk, horizon)

    {
      "tier": "Premium|Growth|Core|Speculative",
      "liquidity": "High|Medium|Low",

D) Hard “anti-fluff” enforcement (simple trick)

Add this to your prompt right before generation:

If any thesis bullet could apply to 10+ random players, rewrite it to be more specific OR mark the missing input as Unknown and use conditional language.

That one line cuts generic output dramatically.

4) What to do with your existing Market Outlook feature

You don’t kill it — you reposition it so you’re not competing head-on.

A) Rename it so expectations change

Market Outlook (current) becomes:

Price Evidence

Card Comps Snapshot

Supporting Evidence

Language matters. You’re telling users:

“This supports the thesis — it’s not the thesis.”

B) Move it to a secondary role everywhere

On Player Outlook: keep it collapsed as you already did (perfect)

On Card pages: show Player Outlook first, then “Card Evidence”

C) Change the goal of comps

Stop trying to be a full price engine. Make comps answer only:

“Is there enough liquidity here to exit?”

“Are prices moving up or down recently?”

“Is this data thin / noisy / confident?”

That’s the right use of your current engine.

D) Use your current comps engine for one thing it can win at

Entry timing guidance. Even thin comps can support:

“Don’t buy immediately after spikes”

“This player’s cards tend to retrace after big games”

“Low-liquidity cards have wide spreads”

So Market Outlook becomes the evidence layer that strengthens the player thesis.

E) Simple new framing (UI copy)

At the top of the evidence panel:

“We use comps as supporting evidence — not as the decision driver.”

That sentence prevents “why aren’t you Market Movers?” comparisons.
