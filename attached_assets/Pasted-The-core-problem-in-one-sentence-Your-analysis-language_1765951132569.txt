The core problem (in one sentence)

Your analysis language is explaining scores,
when it should be telling a story about the card.

Collectors don’t want:

“low demand and high market friction indicating caution”

They want:

“This card is hard to move quickly, and prices vary a lot based on eye appeal.”

Same truth. Totally different feel.

The solution: switch from “analysis voice” → “editorial voice”

You do this with three changes:

Stop describing metrics

Start describing situations

Anchor everything to how collectors actually talk

1. Kill abstract nouns (this is the #1 fix)

Words to aggressively remove:

indicating

reflecting

coupled with

necessitates

warranted

volatility

liquidity (as a noun)

risk (without qualifier)

These words are dead giveaways of bot-speak.

Replace them with:

sells slowly

trades infrequently

prices are spread out

hard to move quickly

no clear catalyst

stable over time

mostly flat

Rule for Replit:

If a sentence contains more than one abstract noun, rewrite it.

2. Move from “because X + Y” → “what that means in real life”
Current pattern (bot-like)

“The recommendation to WATCH reflects low demand and high market friction.”

Editorial pattern

“This card trades infrequently and prices vary widely, making it better suited for patient collectors than short-term moves.”

Notice:

No scores

No math

No system language

Just reality

3. Write in collector archetypes, not system states

Every card fits one of a few collector situations.

Your language should reflect that, not the algorithm.

Common archetypes you already have:

Legacy / PC card

Junk wax hold

Speculative rookie

Event-driven upside

Illiquid niche card

Each archetype should have its own tone.

Example: Rewrite your Maravich analysis
What you have now

“The recommendation to WATCH is due to low demand and high market friction, indicating caution is warranted.”

What it should sound like

“This is a classic vintage card with proven long-term appeal. Sales are infrequent and prices vary widely based on eye appeal, so it’s best viewed as a personal collection hold rather than a short-term trade.”

That sentence:

Sounds human

Sounds confident

Sounds like a collector, not a dashboard

4. Use sentence starters that force editorial tone

Give Replit a fixed set of sentence openers.

These naturally steer language away from bot-speak.

Strong starters to use:

“This card tends to…”

“Collectors typically see this as…”

“Prices for this card usually…”

“Historically, this card has…”

“This is best suited for…”

“This isn’t a card people flip quickly…”

“Value here is driven more by…”

If the model must start with one of these, the output will sound human.

5. Cap explanations at 2–3 sentences (no more)

Bots ramble. Humans don’t.

Hard rule:

Max 3 sentences

Ideally 2

If you need more, hide it behind “Read more (Pro)”.

6. Tie language to the recommendation type

Each recommendation should have a distinct editorial voice.

BUY

Opportunity-focused

Calm, not hypey

Example:

“Recent prices have pulled back despite steady demand, creating a decent entry point if you’re looking to add this card.”

WATCH (Speculative)

Neutral, observational

Example:

“Prices have been inconsistent and there’s no clear trend yet, making this one worth monitoring rather than acting on.”

LEGACY / PC HOLD

Confident, reassuring

Example:

“This card has held value over time and trades infrequently. It’s better viewed as a long-term personal collection piece than a timing play.”

SELL

Matter-of-fact, not alarmist

Example:

“Recent price spikes have pushed this card well above its usual range, making this a reasonable window to take profits.”

7. Tell Replit to stop explaining the system entirely

This is critical.

Never explain why the algorithm chose something.
Only explain what’s happening in the market.

Bad:

“Based on computed upside and risk scores…”

Good:

“Recent sales show prices have climbed quickly, while buyer interest appears to be cooling.”

The system should be invisible.
